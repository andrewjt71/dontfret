Guitar Interval Training

Understanding intervals is crucial for building chords, constructing scales, and developing your ear.

Intervals are fundamental to understanding how chords are constructed. At their core, chords are built by stacking specific intervals on top of a root note. For example, a major chord consists of a root note, plus a major 3rd interval, plus a perfect 5th interval above the root. This understanding of intervals allows you to build, name, and modify chords - whether you're turning a major chord into a minor one or adding a seventh to create more complex harmonies. The relationship between intervals and scales is equally important. Scales are essentially sequences of notes arranged at specific interval distances from each other. Take the major scale as an example - it follows a pattern of whole steps and half steps (W-W-H-W-W-W-H) that creates its characteristic sound. Once you understand these interval relationships, you can construct any scale starting from any root note. Intervals are also crucial for navigating the fretboard effectively. The beauty of intervals is that they maintain consistent patterns across the guitar neck (with a slight adjustment needed between the G and B strings due to the guitar's standard tuning). By learning these interval patterns, you gain the ability to play arpeggios, scales, and chords fluently anywhere on the neck. Perhaps one of the most practical applications of interval knowledge is in transposition. When you understand a musical phrase or chord progression in terms of its intervals, you can easily move it to any key by shifting the entire pattern up or down the neck while maintaining the same interval relationships. This skill is invaluable for adapting songs to different vocal ranges or playing in different keys with other musicians.

Our interval training mode helps you recognize the distance between notes, from minor 2nds to perfect octaves. This knowledge is fundamental for chord construction and scale building.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is guitar interval training?

Guitar interval training helps you recognize the distance between two notes. Intervals are the building blocks of chords and scales, making them essential for understanding music theory and improving your guitar playing.

How does chord training improve my guitar playing?

Chord training teaches you to identify the individual notes that make up different chord types. This knowledge helps you understand chord construction, enables you to create your own chord voicings, and improves your ability to learn new songs.

Why is note training important for guitarists?

Note training helps you memorize the location of every note on the guitar fretboard. This fundamental skill is crucial for understanding music theory, learning scales, building chords, and developing your improvisation skills.

Is this guitar learning tool suitable for beginners?

Yes! Don't Fret It is designed for guitarists of all levels. Beginners can start with note training on individual strings, while more advanced players can practice complex chord constructions and interval recognition.

How can guitar teachers use this tool?

Guitar teachers can use Don't Fret It as a supplementary teaching tool to reinforce music theory concepts. The interactive nature makes it perfect for homework assignments or in-lesson practice sessions.

What makes this guitar tool different from others?

Unlike static diagrams or videos, Don't Fret It provides interactive, hands-on practice. You get immediate feedback on your answers, making the learning process more engaging and effective than traditional methods.

Chord Training Guide

Chord training helps you understand the building blocks of harmony. Each chord type has a specific formula:

Major Root + M3 + P5 Root + Major 3rd + Perfect 5th (e.g., C major = C, E, G)
Minor Root + m3 + P5 Root + Minor 3rd + Perfect 5th (e.g., C minor = C, D#, G)
Diminished Root + m3 + d5 Root + Minor 3rd + Diminished 5th (e.g., C diminished = C, D#, F#)
Augmented Root + M3 + A5 Root + Major 3rd + Augmented 5th (e.g., C augmented = C, E, G#)
Major 7th Root + M3 + P5 + M7 Root + Major 3rd + Perfect 5th + Major 7th (e.g., C major 7 = C, E, G, B)
Minor 7th Root + m3 + P5 + m7 Root + Minor 3rd + Perfect 5th + Minor 7th (e.g., C minor 7 = C, D#, G, A#)
Dominant 7th Root + M3 + P5 + m7 Root + Major 3rd + Perfect 5th + Minor 7th (e.g., C dominant 7 = C, E, G, A#)
Diminished 7th Root + m3 + d5 + d7 Root + Minor 3rd + Diminished 5th + Diminished 7th (e.g., C diminished 7 = C, D#, F#, A)

Scale Training Guide

Scale training helps you understand the building blocks of melody and improvisation. Each scale type has a specific pattern of intervals:

Major W-W-H-W-W-W-H The foundation scale (e.g., C major = C, D, E, F, G, A, B)
Natural Minor W-H-W-W-H-W-W Relative minor scale (e.g., A minor = A, B, C, D, E, F, G)
Harmonic Minor W-H-W-W-H-WH-H Minor scale with raised 7th (e.g., A harmonic minor = A, B, C, D, E, F, G#)
Melodic Minor W-H-W-W-W-W-H Minor scale with raised 6th & 7th ascending (e.g., A melodic minor = A, B, C, D, E, F#, G#)
Major Pentatonic 5-note scale Major scale without 4th and 7th (e.g., C major pentatonic = C, D, E, G, A)
Minor Pentatonic 5-note scale Minor scale without 2nd and 6th (e.g., A minor pentatonic = A, C, D, E, G)
Blues 6-note scale Minor pentatonic with blue note (e.g., A blues = A, C, D, D#, E, G)
Dorian W-H-W-W-W-H-W Minor scale with major 6th (e.g., D dorian = D, E, F, G, A, B, C)
Mixolydian W-W-H-W-W-H-W Major scale with minor 7th (e.g., G mixolydian = G, A, B, C, D, E, F)
Lydian W-W-W-H-W-W-H Major scale with raised 4th (e.g., F lydian = F, G, A, B, C, D, E)
Phrygian H-W-W-W-H-W-W Minor scale with minor 2nd (e.g., E phrygian = E, F, G, A, B, C, D)
Locrian H-W-W-H-W-W-W Minor scale with minor 2nd and diminished 5th (e.g., B locrian = B, C, D, E, F, G, A)
